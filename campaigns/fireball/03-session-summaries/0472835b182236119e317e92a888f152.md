## Highlighted Scene 1: Atramir + Vlita — Warding Bond Dragon Fight

**Setup:** Wizard Atramir casts Warding Bond on dragonknight Vlita before a dragon fight. By spell mechanics, every hit Vlita takes also damages Atramir. They fight the dragon together with this literal shared-pain link.

**Beats:**
- Atramir casts Warding Bond: *"She feels safe from the perils of the world as she feels her resolve harden, as if as long as she's side by side with him she can take on anything thrown at her."*
- Vlita leads the charge; Atramir absorbs half of every hit she takes, visible in HP totals
- Dragon focuses on Vlita; Atramir flinches but stays up; continues casting
- Vlita smiles at Atramir mid-fight
- Dragon falls; Atramir is significantly wounded despite not having been directly attacked
- The warmth in the aftermath comes from both knowing why

**Why it works for comics:** Warding Bond is the rarest kind of comic material — a mechanical D&D rule that *is* a metaphor. A character choosing to share damage with another person is literalized onscreen: every hit on Vlita produces a simultaneous wince panel on Atramir. The fight structure is already there (setup → escalating hits shared → smile → victory → wordless aftermath). The complete arc (Bond cast → fight → victory) is 6–8 pages with natural breathing room.

## Highlighted Scene 2: Dôrìqûe — Mercy Killing

**Setup:** Dôrìqûe encounters creatures made of crystallized despair. They beg to be killed. They speak in children's voices.

**Beats:**
- Creature: *"I'm so sorry... I didn't mean to!"*
- Another: *"Why...kill us again... Dorique..."*
- Dôrìqûe kills them one by one with no combat challenge — it is not a fight, it is an act of mercy
- No resolution, no comfort; Dôrìqûe moves on

**Why it works for comics:** A single dark page. The children's voices create horror without explanation. Works as a tonal contrast insert — before or after a lighter scene.
